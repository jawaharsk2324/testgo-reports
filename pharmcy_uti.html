Additional Information

Gardnerella vaginalis can be pathogenic when found in urine samples. Resistance genes were detected in MecA which may limit available treatment options. Tinidazole efficacy against Gardnerella vaginalis is uncertain. The primary drug for treatment is Metronidazole, with a dosage of 500 mg PO BID for 7 days.

Treatment Timing Guidelines

Asymptomatic bacteriuria does not typically need treatment, and microbe detection may not indicate infection. In this case, Gardnerella vaginalis, typically associated with bacterial vaginosis, has been detected. The primary drug, Metronidazole, is typically administered for 7 days. In more complicated cases or if multiple organisms were detected, therapy may be extended to 7-14 days.

Special Considerations

Patients may have colonized microbes, so a positive specimen does not necessarily mean infection. Gardnerella vaginalis may have intrinsic resistance to certain antimicrobials, making it difficult to treat. This organism may require modified dosing and duration. Resistance is variable; therefore, drug failure is possible. As MecA resistance is on the CDC threat list, tracking and monitoring may be indicated if possible. Antibiotics should therefore be used with caution as drug failure is possible.

Methodology

Nucleic acids are extracted from urine samples and analyzed using Real-Time Polymerase Chain Reaction (RT-PCR). The Genepath-MGN-LUTI assays detect urinary tract pathogens and antibiotic resistance markers with greater than 99% accuracy and specificity. Infection ranges—classified as high, medium, or low—were established during method validation based on corresponding CFU/μL values. The clinical significance of these ranges has not been evaluated by MicroGen.
